One in ten women faces polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS) during her lifetime. Learn about this condition, its common symptoms, and how it's managed.

What is PCOS?

PCOS is a common hormonal condition that is thought to impact between 8% and 13% of women of reproductive age. That's around 5 million people. However, you might be curious as to what we mean by a hormonal condition. So, let's break PCOS down. PCOS is a condition that affects your hormone levels and usually causes an imbalance in your androgen levels, sometimes called male hormones. This imbalance can impact how often you ovulate (when your ovaries release an egg for fertilization) and how frequent your periods are. Sometimes people who have PCOS have multiple small cysts that form on their ovaries. However, surprisingly, this isn't always the case. You could be diagnosed with PCOS and never have cysts on your ovaries.

Common PCOS symptoms checklist

If you experience symptoms of polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS), they'll usually become apparent in your late teens or early 20s.

Missed or irregular periods

Having few menstrual periods or having periods that aren't regular are common signs of PCOS. So is having periods that last for many days or longer than is typical for a period.

Excessive hair growth or hair loss

You might find that dark or coarse hair grows on your arms, chest, face, or abdomen, this excessive hair growth is called hirsutism.

Anxiety or depression

Insomnia or excessive sleep, along with changes in appetite and weight, can be symptoms of both depression and PCOS. Both conditions can also cause mood fluctuations, periods of low mood or sadness, and chronic fatigue or low energy levels.

Polycystic Ovaries

On ultrasound, the ovaries may appear enlarged and have small fluid-filled sacs or follicles, giving them a polycystic appearance. However, not all individuals with PCOS will have polycystic ovaries, and the presence of cysts alone is not sufficient for a diagnosis.
